How GPA Is Calculated

GPA (Grade Point Average) is calculated by multiplying each course's grade points by its credit hours, summing all those products, and dividing by total credit hours. This is a weighted average — a 4-credit course counts twice as much as a 2-credit course.

GPA Formula GPA = ∑(Grade Points × Credit Hours) / ∑(Credit Hours)
A = 4.0   A− = 3.7   B+ = 3.3   B = 3.0   B− = 2.7 C+ = 2.3   C = 2.0   C− = 1.7   D = 1.0   F = 0.0

Understanding the 4.0 Scale

Most US colleges use a 4.0 scale where A = 4.0 and F = 0.0. Some schools use a 4.33 scale where A+ = 4.33, but many cap A+ at 4.0. For graduate school applications, most programs prefer a GPA of 3.0 or above (B average); competitive programs often require 3.5+. Medical and law schools typically want 3.5–4.0.

Semester vs Cumulative GPA

Semester GPA only includes courses from the current term — useful for measuring one semester's performance. Cumulative GPA averages all courses taken throughout your college career. Admissions, scholarships, and honors programs typically evaluate cumulative GPA. Your semester GPA can pull your cumulative up or down depending on how it compares to your cumulative average.

What GPA Do You Need for Grad School?

  • Top-tier PhD programs / medical school: 3.7–4.0
  • Competitive master's programs: 3.3–3.7
  • Standard graduate admission: 3.0 minimum at most schools
  • MBA programs (top schools): 3.5+ average for admitted students
  • Law school (T14): 3.7+ is competitive

Can You Raise Your GPA Significantly?

GPA is slow to change due to accumulated credit hours. With 60 credits at 2.8, even getting straight A's (4.0) for the next 30 credits only brings your cumulative GPA to: (2.8×60 + 4.0×30) / 90 = 3.2. Start improving immediately — the earlier in your academic career, the more leverage you have. Use this calculator to simulate the impact of different grade scenarios in upcoming courses.

How do I calculate my GPA from letter grades? ›

Convert each letter grade to grade points (A=4.0, B=3.0, C=2.0, D=1.0, F=0.0, with +/− adjustments of ±0.3), then multiply each by the course's credit hours. Sum all those products, then divide by total credit hours. Example: English (3 credits, A = 4.0) + Math (4 credits, B+ = 3.3) + History (3 credits, A− = 3.7). Totals: (4.0×3) + (3.3×4) + (3.7×3) = 12 + 13.2 + 11.1 = 36.3 quality points ÷ 10 credit hours = 3.63 GPA. Enter your courses above for automatic calculation.

What is a good GPA in college? ›

Context matters, but general benchmarks: 3.7–4.0 is excellent (Dean's List, magna/summa cum laude territory). 3.3–3.7 is very good (strong candidate for most graduate programs). 3.0–3.3 is above average (meets minimum requirements for most grad schools). 2.5–3.0 is average (some employers and programs have 3.0 minimum requirements). Below 2.5 may limit opportunities. Note: GPA standards vary by major — a 3.0 in engineering or pre-med is often more respected than a 3.8 in an easier major.

How is a weighted GPA different from unweighted? ›

The most common college GPA is weighted by credit hours — a 4-credit course counts more than a 2-credit course (this is what this calculator computes). Some high schools use a different type of "weighted" GPA that gives extra points for AP or honors courses (e.g., A in AP = 5.0 instead of 4.0). College GPA almost always means credit-hour weighted on a 4.0 scale — not the AP-style weighting. When someone asks about your college GPA, they mean the credit-weighted 4.0 scale version.

What GPA do I need to graduate with honors? ›

Graduation honor thresholds vary by institution, but common standards: Cum Laude ("with honor") typically requires 3.5–3.6+ GPA. Magna Cum Laude ("with great honor") requires 3.7–3.8+. Summa Cum Laude ("with highest honor") requires 3.9–4.0. Some schools use a class rank system instead of fixed GPA thresholds (e.g., top 10% = Cum Laude, top 5% = Magna, top 2% = Summa). Check your school's specific requirements — they vary widely. Enter your current and projected grades above to see if you can reach your target threshold.

Does a bad freshman GPA ruin my chances for grad school? ›

Not necessarily. Graduate admissions committees often look at grade trends, and a strong upward trajectory from a weak start demonstrates resilience and growth. Strategies: (1) Earn consistently high grades for the rest of college to maximize cumulative GPA improvement. (2) Prepare for and excel on standardized tests (GRE/GMAT) to demonstrate ability. (3) Get strong recommendation letters from professors who know your work well. (4) Address your freshman performance directly in your personal statement if relevant. (5) Consider a post-bac academic record program to prove consistent academic capability at a higher level.
